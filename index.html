§ 09 — EPILOGUE: LEGAL FALLOUT
FROM THE
BOOTH TO
THE BENCH

When you can't win in rap, take it to court. Drake's 117-page lawsuit against Universal Music Group began a First Amendment battle that could redefine hip-hop's legal landscape forever.

NOV 2024
Kendrick Drops GNX
Surprise-released his sixth studio album. Capitalizing on astronomical post-feud momentum. Drake released Some Sexy Songs 4 U with PARTYNEXTDOOR — commercial survival mode.
JAN 2025
Drake Files 117-Page Lawsuit
Filed against his own distributor, Universal Music Group. Drake alleged UMG "intentionally published and promoted" "Not Like Us" knowing the pedophilia and sex trafficking allegations were false and defamatory. The rap war becomes a legal war.
OCT 2025
Federal Judge Dismisses the Case
Judge Jeannette A. Vargas ruled the lyrics constituted "hyperbolic vituperations" and "protected opinion" within "perhaps the most infamous rap battle in the genre's history." Not actionable statements of verifiable fact.
EARLY 2026
Drake Appeals to the Second Circuit
Refuses to accept dismissal. Escalates to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. UMG fires back with an 83-page appellate brief — accusing Drake of "astounding hypocrisy" given his own unverified allegations in "Family Matters."
APR 2026
Yale, Howard, Tulane Weigh In
The Floyd Abrams Institute for Freedom of Expression at Yale Law School files amicus briefs supporting UMG. A coalition from Howard University and Tulane emphasizes hip-hop's foundational reliance on hyperbole. The outcome will reshape how courts interpret the genre forever.
The UMG Counter-Argument
UMG highlighted Drake's "astounding hypocrisy" — noting he used UMG platforms to levy unverified domestic abuse allegations against Lamar, and that he actively provoked attacks using AI-generated voices of Tupac Shakur.
The Yale Position
The Floyd Abrams Institute argued ruling in Drake's favor would "critically undermine a highly creative art form built on exaggeration, insult, and wordplay." Hip-hop as protected speech — the stakes could not be higher.
What's At Stake
The 2026 appellate case stands to fundamentally reshape how courts interpret hip-hop lyrics. The genre's inherent competitiveness — its DNA — may be ruled either protected artistic expression or subject to strict defamation liability.
